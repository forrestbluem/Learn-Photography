LESSON 01

Composition & the Rule of Thirds

🎯

Composition is how you arrange everything inside your photo. It's the difference between a snapshot and a photograph that makes people stop and look. The most important rule to learn first is the Rule of Thirds.

Imagine your viewfinder split into 9 equal boxes by two horizontal and two vertical lines. The four points where the lines cross are the power points. Place your main subject on one of them and your photo becomes immediately more dynamic and interesting.

The Rule of Thirds Grid

Place your subject on a power point (gold dots) — not dead centre

📷
On Your Nikon DX
Turn on the Rule of Thirds grid right inside your viewfinder: press MENU → Custom Settings (pencil icon) → d Shooting/Display → d8 Viewfinder Grid Display → ON. Now when you look through the viewfinder you'll see the grid lines while composing. You can also see it on the rear LCD Live View by pressing the info button repeatedly until the grid appears.

Other great composition techniques to try alongside Rule of Thirds:

🌅
Horizons
Put the horizon on the top or bottom third. Never let it cut the photo perfectly in half.
➡️
Leading Lines
Roads, rivers, fences — lines that draw the viewer's eye deep into the scene.
🖼️
Natural Framing
Use doorways, archways, or tree branches to frame your subject like a picture frame.
🔵
Foreground Interest
Adding something close in the foreground gives depth and pulls viewers into the scene.
📱
On a Phone or Point-and-Shoot
iPhone: Settings → Camera → Grid ON. Android: Camera app → Settings (cog icon) → Grid Lines ON. Point-and-shoot: Press the DISP button (or Info button) until the grid appears on the screen. Same idea — use those lines to compose your shot!

LESSON 02

The Exposure Triangle

☀️

Exposure is how bright or dark your photo turns out. Your Nikon has three settings that control this together — they form the famous Exposure Triangle. Change one, and you usually need to compensate with another. Understanding this is the key to proper camera control!

The Exposure Triangle — three controls, one result
🌡️
ISO
ISO button (top of body)
Sensor sensitivity. Higher = brighter but grainy. Low (100) = clean daylight. High (3200) = noisy but lets you shoot in dark rooms.
⏱️
Shutter Speed
Rear command dial
How long the shutter stays open. Fast (1/1000) freezes action. Slow (1/30) blurs motion. Below 1/60 risks camera shake.
🔍
Aperture
Front sub-command dial
Width of the lens opening. f/1.8 = wide = blurry background. f/16 = narrow = everything sharp. Lower f-number = wider opening.
📷
Setting ISO on Your Nikon DX
Hold the ISO button on the top-left of the body and turn the rear command dial. Watch the ISO number change in your viewfinder or on the top LCD screen.

ISO 100–200 → Bright sunshine outdoors
ISO 400–800 → Overcast or indoor daylight
ISO 1600–3200 → Dim indoors, evening
ISO 6400+ → Very dark — grain will be visible, but sometimes worth it!

Here's how aperture looks — the f-number is the size of the lens opening. Confusingly, a smaller number = a wider opening:

📱
On a Phone or Point-and-Shoot
Phones handle all three automatically. But you can influence them! Tap on a bright area → camera darkens. Tap on a dark area → camera brightens. On iPhone: tap to focus then slide the ☀️ sun icon up or down to adjust brightness. On Android: tap subject, then look for a brightness slider. On a point-and-shoot: look for scene modes — the ⚽ sports icon uses a faster shutter, the 🙂 portrait icon uses a wider aperture.

LESSON 03

The Mode Dial — Your Best Friend

🔢

Look at the top of your Nikon — there's a big dial with letters and symbols. This is the Mode Dial. It decides how much control you have versus how much the camera decides for you. This is where you choose your shooting strategy!

Nikon DX Mode Dial — the gold ones are best to learn on first
AUTO
Full Auto
Camera decides everything. Good for quick snapshots but you learn nothing!
P
Program Auto
Camera picks shutter & aperture — you control ISO, WB, flash and more.
A
Aperture Priority
You set the aperture — camera sets shutter. Best all-round learning mode!
S
Shutter Priority
You set shutter speed — camera sets aperture. Perfect for action & sports.
M
Full Manual
You control everything. Very powerful — work up to this over time.
📷
Start Here: A Mode (Aperture Priority)
Set the dial to A. Turn the sub-command dial (small dial on the front of the grip near the shutter button) to change aperture.

f/2.8 or f/4 → Portraits — beautiful blurry background
f/5.6 or f/8 → General use — sharp subject, some background separation
f/11 or f/16 → Landscapes — everything sharp from front to back

The camera automatically handles the shutter speed to keep the exposure correct. A great starting combination: A mode, f/5.6, ISO 400.
🙂
Portraits
A mode + f/2.8–f/4. Blurry background makes faces really stand out.
⚽
Sport & Action
S mode + 1/500 or faster. Freezes fast movement with no blur.
🏔️
Landscapes
A mode + f/8–f/11. Everything from foreground to horizon is sharp.
🌙
Low Light
A mode + widest aperture + raise ISO to 800–3200. Lets in maximum light.
💡
A great beginner starting point: A mode, f/5.6, ISO Auto or 400. This "neutral" setting works in most daylight. From there, you only need to adjust one thing at a time as needed.
📱
On a Phone or Point-and-Shoot
Many smartphones now have a Pro Mode or Manual Mode in the camera app that lets you adjust ISO and shutter speed — same idea as P/S/A on a DSLR. Point-and-shoot cameras often have a mode dial too — look for P, A/Av, S/Tv — same letters as a Nikon! Scene modes (portrait 🙂, sports ⚽, landscape 🏔️) are the beginner version of these.

LESSON 04

Autofocus & Depth of Field

🎯

Your Nikon's autofocus (AF) system is very clever — but it needs to know what you want sharp and whether your subject is moving. Picking the wrong AF mode is one of the most common reasons photos come out blurry.

📷
Three AF Modes on Your Nikon DX
Find the focus mode selector switch on the front-left of the camera body (near the lens). You can also press the AF button on the rear and turn the command dial.

AF-S (Single-Servo) — Locks focus when you half-press the shutter. Perfect for still subjects. Use for: portraits, flowers, food, buildings.

AF-C (Continuous-Servo) — Keeps adjusting focus while you hold the shutter half-down, tracking a moving subject. Use for: sports, pets, birds in flight.

AF-A (Auto) — Camera switches between S and C automatically. A good safe fallback.
📷
The Half-Press — The Most Important Habit
Never just jab the shutter button — you'll almost always get blur. Instead: press the shutter halfway down first. Wait until you hear a beep and see the green dot light up in the bottom-left of your viewfinder. That confirms focus is locked. Then press fully to take the shot. This one habit alone will dramatically improve your photos.

On a Phone or Point-and-Shoot
On a smartphone, simply tap the screen where you want focus — that's it! For bokeh, use Portrait Mode. Without Portrait Mode: get physically very close to your subject and make sure the background is far away — the distance difference creates natural blur. On a point-and-shoot: the half-press technique is identical to a DSLR — this is a universal camera skill.

LESSON 05

Finding Great Light

💡

Photography literally means "writing with light" in Greek. The single biggest difference between a great photo and a mediocre one is usually the quality of light — not the camera. A phone photo in good light beats a DSLR photo in bad light almost every time.

🌅
Golden Hour
~1 hour after sunrise or before sunset. Soft, warm, long shadows. Professional photographers plan their shoots around this!
🌫️
Blue Hour
Just after sunset — the sky turns deep blue while city lights glow. Magical for cityscapes and architecture.
☁️
Overcast Sky
Clouds act as a giant natural softbox — soft, even, flattering light. Surprisingly great for portraits!
🪟
Window Light
Place a subject beside (not in front of) a window. Free, beautiful, directional light — perfect for portraits.
🌞
Harsh Midday Sun
Hard shadows, squinting subjects. Avoid for portraits — find open shade under a tree instead.
💡
Artificial Light
Lamps, candles, fairy lights — all create mood. Raise ISO and shoot handheld without flash for atmosphere.
📷
Exposure Compensation — Your Secret Weapon
Your Nikon's meter sometimes gets the brightness wrong — especially with bright snow, backlit subjects, or dark rooms. Use the +/– button (top-right of body, near the shutter) and turn the main command dial to adjust:

+1 or +2 → Brighten the photo (use for: snow, backlit subjects, faces in shadow)
–1 or –2 → Darken the photo (use for: sunsets, avoiding blown-out skies)

You'll see the change in real-time in your viewfinder. Always reset it to 0 after!
⚠️
Never shoot with the sun directly behind your subject unless you want a silhouette. The camera exposes for the bright background and your subject's face goes dark. Solution: move so the sun is on their face, or use +2 exposure compensation to lift the shadows.
💡
Window light portrait trick: sit a friend beside a window on a cloudy day. Set your Nikon to A mode, f/2.8, ISO 640. The result will look like a professional studio shot — no studio required!
📱
On a Phone or Point-and-Shoot
iPhone: tap to focus, then slide the ☀️ icon up or down to adjust brightness — this is exposure compensation. Android: tap the subject, look for a brightness slider. Point-and-shoot: press the +/– button — exactly the same concept as on a DSLR! Same problem, same solution, just a different button location.

LESSON 06

Colour, White Balance & Mood

🎨

Colours make us feel things. Warm oranges feel exciting and cosy. Cool blues feel calm and peaceful. Great photographers choose their colours on purpose. Your Nikon has a powerful setting called White Balance that tells the camera what "white" looks like — and it can completely change the mood of your photo.

📷
White Balance on Your Nikon DX
Press the WB button on top of the camera and turn the rear command dial to cycle through these settings:

AUTO — Camera guesses. Good in most situations but can look clinical.
☀️ Direct Sunlight (5500K) — Accurate, neutral colours in sunshine.
☁️ Cloudy (6000K) — Adds a touch of warmth. Great for portraits on grey days.
🔦 Shade (7000K) — Very warm and golden. Try this creatively on any scene!
💡 Tungsten / Incandescent (3200K) — Corrects for orange indoor bulbs, makes colours cooler.

Creative tip: Use "Shade" WB outdoors on a sunny day for a warm, cinematic film look!
Colour Temperature Scale (Kelvin)
2000K
Cool Blue 5500K
Neutral Daylight 10000K
Warm Orange
📷
Picture Controls — Nikon's Built-in Looks
Go to Shooting Menu (camera icon) → Set Picture Control to change how Nikon processes the colours:

Standard — Good balanced all-rounder for everyday shooting.
Vivid — Punchy, saturated colours. Great for landscapes and nature.
Portrait — Softer tones, flattering skin colours.
Landscape — Boosts greens and blues for outdoor scenes.
Monochrome — Black & white in-camera. Dramatic and timeless.
Flat — Low contrast, muted — best if you plan to edit the photo afterwards.
🔥
Warm Tones
Oranges and yellows feel cosy, exciting, energetic. Great for food, portraits, and sunsets.
❄️
Cool Tones
Blues and greens feel calm, peaceful, slightly melancholy. Great for water and landscapes.
🎭
Complementary
Opposite colours pop! Blue sky + orange sunset is the classic combination photographers love.
⬛
Black & White
Removing colour focuses everything on shape, texture and emotion. Powerful for drama and storytelling.
📱
On a Phone or Point-and-Shoot
On iPhone or Android, edit photos after shooting: open a photo → tap Edit → adjust Warmth (makes colours warmer or cooler). The free app Snapseed gives you much more control, including a "Tune Image" tool with warmth, saturation, and more. Point-and-shoots usually have a WB button — try "Cloudy" on a sunny day for instantly warmer, more pleasing colours.
